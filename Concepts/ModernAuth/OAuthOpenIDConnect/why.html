// 01 — Why

Why OAuth exists

Before OAuth, the only way to let an app use your account on another service was to give it your password. That was a terrible idea — and fixing it is the entire reason this protocol was born.

The valet-key analogy

Imagine your car has two keys. The master key opens everything — doors, trunk, glovebox — and lets you drive anywhere. The valet key only starts the engine and opens the door; it can’t open the trunk or glovebox, and you can throw it away after the valet is done.

When you hand your car to a valet, you don’t give them the master key. You give them the limited one. OAuth is the valet key for your online accounts: it lets an app do one specific job, with limited power, for a limited time — without ever seeing your real password.

The “password anti-pattern”

Picture a photo-printing website that offers to print the photos you keep on a separate photo-sharing service. The naïve way to make that work:

the bad old way
printer.example> Please enter your photo-service username & password
  username: you@example.com
  password: ••••••••••••

# the printing site now stores YOUR password and logs in AS YOU
# it can read, delete, or share EVERYTHING — forever

RFC 6749 lists exactly why this is dangerous. Sharing your credentials with a third party means:

ProblemWhat it means for you
Stored passwordsThe third-party app keeps your password around — often in clear text — waiting to be stolen in a breach.
Over-broad accessIt gets everything your account can do. No way to say “only my photos, not my messages.”
No time limitAccess lasts until you change your password. There’s no “expires in 1 hour.”
All-or-nothing revocationTo cut off one app you must change your password — which breaks every other app too.
Blast radiusIf that one app is hacked, your password — and every service that shares it — is compromised.

Source: RFC 6749, §1 (Introduction).

OAuth’s big idea: separate the roles

The breakthrough in OAuth is almost philosophical: stop treating “the app” and “the user” as the same entity. The spec puts it this way —

// RFC 6749 §1

“OAuth addresses these issues by introducing an authorization layer and separating the role of the client from that of the resource owner… the client obtains an access token — a string denoting a specific scope, lifetime, and other access attributes — instead of using the resource owner’s credentials.”

So instead of your password, the app receives a token: a limited, expiring, revocable permission slip. The photo printer gets something that effectively says “may read this person’s photos until 4pm today, nothing else.”

❌ Password sharing

  • Full account access
  • Never expires
  • Can’t scope it down
  • Revoke = change password

✅ OAuth token

  • Only the access you approved (scope)
  • Expires automatically
  • Granular per app & permission
  • Revoke one app, keep the rest

“But isn’t this just logging in?” — authz vs. authn

This trips up almost everyone, so let’s nail it down:

TermQuestion it answersHandled by
Authentication (authn)“Who are you?”OpenID Connect
Authorization (authz)“What are you allowed to do?”OAuth 2.0

OAuth 2.0 was designed for authorization — delegated access to resources. People then started (mis)using it to log users in, which is subtly different and led to security pitfalls. That’s exactly why OpenID Connect was created: to do authentication properly, on top of OAuth. We cover it on the OIDC page.

Common myth

“OAuth logs me in.” Not by itself. Plain OAuth tells an app what it can access, not who you are. The login part is OpenID Connect.

Key takeaways

  • Sharing your password with third-party apps is dangerous — the “password anti-pattern.”
  • OAuth replaces password-sharing with scoped, expiring, revocable tokens.
  • It works by separating the user (resource owner) from the app (client).
  • OAuth = authorization (“what can this app do?”). Authentication (“who are you?”) is OpenID Connect.
