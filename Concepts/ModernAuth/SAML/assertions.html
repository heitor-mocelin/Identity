Inside a SAML assertion

The assertion is the whole point of SAML — the signed XML document that says “this is who they are, here’s how they logged in, and here’s what we know about them.” Let’s open one up.

The five parts at a glance

Every assertion is built from a predictable set of elements. Here they are before we see the XML:

ElementAnswers
<Issuer>Who minted this assertion? (the IdP’s identifier)
<Signature>Proof it’s authentic and untampered (XML Digital Signature)
<Subject>Who is this about? (the NameID) + how the SP confirms it
<Conditions>When & for whom is it valid? (time window + audience)
<AuthnStatement>How & when did they authenticate?
<AttributeStatement>What do we know about them? (email, role, groups…)

Visually, an assertion nests like this — a signed envelope wrapping who, when, how, and what:

<saml:Assertion>

Issuerwho issued it

The Identity Provider that minted this assertion.

SignatureXML-DSig seal

Cryptographic proof of authenticity & integrity — verified with the IdP’s certificate.

Subject → NameIDwho

Identifies the user, plus SubjectConfirmation (recipient, expiry, InResponseTo).

Conditionswhen / for whom

NotBefore / NotOnOrAfter window + AudienceRestriction naming the one valid SP.

AuthnStatementhow they logged in

When & with what strength the user authenticated (e.g. password, MFA).

AttributeStatementwhat we know

Facts about the user: email, role, groups — used by the SP for authorization.

A real assertion, annotated

assertion.xml (inside the SAMLResponse)
<saml:Assertion Version="2.0" ID="_x9f..."
    IssueInstant="2026-06-19T19:00:05Z">

  <saml:Issuer>https://idp.example.com/saml</saml:Issuer>     # who issued it

  <ds:Signature> ...digest + RSA signature... </ds:Signature>     # tamper-proof seal

  <saml:Subject>                                            # WHO this is about
    <saml:NameID Format="...emailAddress">ada@example.com</saml:NameID>
    <saml:SubjectConfirmation Method="...bearer">
      <saml:SubjectConfirmationData
          Recipient="https://app.example.com/acs"
          NotOnOrAfter="2026-06-19T19:05:05Z"
          InResponseTo="_a1b2c3"/>           # ties back to the AuthnRequest
    </saml:SubjectConfirmation>
  </saml:Subject>

  <saml:Conditions                                          # WHEN / FOR WHOM it's valid
      NotBefore="2026-06-19T19:00:00Z"
      NotOnOrAfter="2026-06-19T19:05:00Z">
    <saml:AudienceRestriction>
      <saml:Audience>https://app.example.com/saml</saml:Audience>  # intended SP
    </saml:AudienceRestriction>
  </saml:Conditions>

  <saml:AuthnStatement AuthnInstant="2026-06-19T19:00:04Z">  # HOW they logged in
    <saml:AuthnContext>
      <saml:AuthnContextClassRef>...PasswordProtectedTransport</saml:AuthnContextClassRef>
    </saml:AuthnContext>
  </saml:AuthnStatement>

  <saml:AttributeStatement>                                # WHAT we know about them
    <saml:Attribute Name="email"><saml:AttributeValue>ada@example.com</saml:AttributeValue></saml:Attribute>
    <saml:Attribute Name="role"><saml:AttributeValue>finance-admin</saml:AttributeValue></saml:Attribute>
  </saml:AttributeStatement>

</saml:Assertion>

Why each part matters

Subject & NameID — “who”

The NameID identifies the user. Its Format matters: an email is human-friendly but changes; a persistent opaque ID is stable and privacy-preserving; a transient ID is throwaway per-session. Pick the most stable identifier your app can key on.

SubjectConfirmation — “and here’s the proof it’s for you”

Most SAML SSO uses the bearer method: whoever presents the assertion is treated as the subject. That’s why the surrounding checks — Recipient, NotOnOrAfter, and InResponseTo — are essential to stop a stolen assertion being replayed elsewhere.

Conditions — “when & for whom”

The NotBefore/NotOnOrAfter window keeps assertions short-lived (often a few minutes). The AudienceRestriction names the one SP this assertion is for — so an assertion minted for App A can’t be used at App B.

AuthnStatement — “how they logged in”

The AuthnContextClassRef tells the SP the strength of authentication — e.g. password over TLS, or multi-factor. A security-sensitive SP can require a stronger context.

AttributeStatement — “what we know”

The carrier for user data the SP needs: email, display name, department, group/role. The SP turns these into its own authorization decisions (e.g. “role = finance-admin → show the approvals screen”).

The signature: SAML’s foundation of trust

An assertion is only trustworthy because it’s digitally signed using XML Signature (XML-DSig). The IdP signs with its private key; the SP verifies with the IdP’s public certificate (from metadata). This guarantees two things:

🔏 Integrity

If even one character changed in transit, signature verification fails.

🪪 Authenticity

Only the real IdP holds the private key, so a valid signature proves origin.

Verify the right thing

An IdP may sign the Response, the Assertion, or both. SPs must verify the signature over the element they actually trust and reject anything unsigned. Getting this subtly wrong is the root of the famous “signature wrapping” attacks — see the Security chapter.

The SP’s validation checklist

Before trusting an assertion, a correct SP checks all of these:

  • ✅ Signature is valid and made by the expected IdP’s certificate
  • ✅ Issuer is the IdP you trust
  • ✅ Audience matches this SP
  • ✅ Current time is within NotBefore … NotOnOrAfter
  • ✅ Recipient is this SP’s ACS URL
  • ✅ InResponseTo matches a request this SP actually sent (SP-initiated)
  • ✅ The assertion hasn’t been seen before (replay protection)
