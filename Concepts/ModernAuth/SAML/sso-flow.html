The SSO flow, step by step

This is the Web Browser SSO Profile — the dance you trigger every time you open a SAML-connected app. It’s mostly redirects and one auto-submitting form. Let’s walk it slowly.

Two ways to start

🅰️ SP-initiated

You go straight to the app first (e.g. open the expense tool). The app notices you’re not logged in and bounces you to the IdP. The most common case.

🅱️ IdP-initiated

You start at a company portal/dashboard, click an app tile, and the IdP pushes an assertion to the app. No AuthnRequest — the IdP sends an unsolicited Response.

SP-initiated SSO — the walk-through

Watch the whole handshake, then read each step in detail:

1

You visit the app

You open the Service Provider (the expense tool). It checks for a session and finds none.

2

SP builds an AuthnRequest and redirects you to the IdP

Using the HTTP-Redirect binding, the request is deflated, Base64- and URL-encoded into a SAMLRequest query parameter. A RelayState remembers where you were headed.

3

You authenticate at the IdP

If you don’t already have an IdP session, you log in here — password, MFA, whatever the IdP enforces. The SP never sees these credentials.

4

IdP builds a signed Response with an assertion

It creates an assertion about you (who you are + attributes), signs it, and wraps it in a SAML Response.

5

IdP returns it via an auto-posting form (HTTP-POST binding)

The IdP sends back an HTML page with a hidden SAMLResponse field that JavaScript auto-submits to the SP’s Assertion Consumer Service (ACS) URL.

6

SP verifies the signature & logs you in

The SP checks the signature against the IdP’s known certificate, validates conditions (audience, timing), reads your identity + attributes, creates a local session, and sends you to your original destination via RelayState. You’re in. 🎉

What the wire looks like

Step 2 — the redirect to the IdP (HTTP-Redirect binding). The SAMLRequest is a compressed, encoded blob; decoded, the XML inside reads like this:

1 — redirect to IdP (decoded AuthnRequest)
GET https://idp.example.com/sso?SAMLRequest=fVLB...%3D&RelayState=/reports

# the decoded SAMLRequest XML:
<samlp:AuthnRequest
    ID="_a1b2c3" Version="2.0"
    IssueInstant="2026-06-19T19:00:00Z"
    Destination="https://idp.example.com/sso"
    AssertionConsumerServiceURL="https://app.example.com/acs">
  <saml:Issuer>https://app.example.com/saml</saml:Issuer>
</samlp:AuthnRequest>

Step 5 — the IdP’s auto-submitting form (HTTP-POST binding):

2 — IdP → browser → SP (auto-posted form)
<form method="post" action="https://app.example.com/acs">
  <input type="hidden" name="SAMLResponse" value="PHNhbWxw..."/>
  <input type="hidden" name="RelayState" value="/reports"/>
</form>
# <script> auto-submits this form to the SP's ACS endpoint

The decoded SAMLResponse contains the signed assertion — we dissect it in the next chapter.

IdP-initiated SSO (and why it’s riskier)

In the IdP-initiated case there’s no AuthnRequest: the IdP simply POSTs an unsolicited Response to the SP’s ACS. It’s convenient for portal dashboards, but because the SP never asked for it, it’s harder to defend against replay and certain injection attacks.

Heads-up

Prefer SP-initiated SSO where you can. Because the SP generated a request with a unique ID, it can correlate the response to it (via InResponseTo) — a check that’s impossible for unsolicited IdP-initiated responses. More on the Security page.

Single Logout (SLO)

SSO’s mirror image: SAML also defines Single Logout, where ending your IdP session can propagate logout to all the SPs you signed into. It’s powerful but notoriously tricky to implement reliably across many apps, so support varies in practice.
