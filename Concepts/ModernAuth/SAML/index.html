// SAML 2.0, the language of enterprise single sign-on

One login for everything at work.

When you sign in once at your company and suddenly have access to email, chat, HR, and a dozen other apps — without logging into each — that’s often SAML at work. This guide explains why it exists, how the XML & redirects fit together, and what each piece does, from everyday analogies down to the OASIS spec.

The 30-second version

SAML (Security Assertion Markup Language) is an open standard that lets a trusted Identity Provider vouch for who you are to many separate applications. Instead of each app checking your password, it accepts a signed XML document — an assertion — from the provider it trusts. Log in once, use everything: Single Sign-On (SSO).

SAML = “a trusted party vouches for you, in signed XML” Released 2005 · OASIS standard

The big picture

Three players, one trusted handshake. Here’s the shape before we name the parts:

saml-sso — the 10,000-foot view
   YOU (the principal / subject)
    │  1. try to open an app
    ▼
 SERVICE PROVIDER (the app)  ──2. "who is this? go authenticate"──▶
    ▲                                                              │
    │  4. accepts signed assertion, logs you in                    ▼
    │                                                  IDENTITY PROVIDER
    └──────────3. signed SAML assertion ◀── you log in here (once) ─┘
