// 01 — Why

Why SAML exists

Before federated single sign-on, every app was its own island — its own password, its own login page, its own copy of your account. SAML was built to connect those islands with trust instead of yet more passwords.

The hotel key-card analogy

Check into a hotel and the front desk verifies your ID once, then hands you a key card. That card opens your room, the gym, the pool, the lounge — without re-proving who you are at each door. Each door just trusts cards issued by the front desk.

SAML works the same way. You authenticate once at a central Identity Provider (the front desk). It issues a signed “key card” — an assertion — that every connected application (each door) trusts, because they trust the issuer. That’s federated single sign-on.

The problem: password & account sprawl

Imagine a company with 80 SaaS tools. Without SSO, every employee needs an account and password in each one. That creates a familiar mess:

ProblemWhat it means in practice
Password fatigueDozens of credentials → people reuse weak passwords or write them down.
Helpdesk cost“I forgot my password” is one of the highest-volume IT tickets, multiplied per app.
Slow on/off-boardingA new hire must be created in 80 systems; a leaver must be disabled in 80 systems — easy to miss one.
Inconsistent securityEach app enforces its own (often weaker) password and MFA rules.
No central controlNo single place to revoke access, audit logins, or enforce policy.

The idea: separate “who you are” from “the app”

SAML’s core move is federation: let identity live in one trusted place and have apps delegate authentication to it. The app never sees your password — it only sees a signed statement from a provider it already trusts.

// the shift

From “every app stores and checks your password” → to “one identity provider authenticates you, and vouches for you to every app.”

❌ Per-app passwords

  • A password in every app
  • 80 places to off-board a leaver
  • MFA only where each app supports it
  • No central audit

✅ SAML federation

  • One login at the Identity Provider
  • Disable one account → access gone everywhere
  • Enforce MFA & policy in one place
  • Central logging of every sign-on

Why XML, and why 2005?

SAML 2.0 was standardised by OASIS in 2005, in the era of enterprise web applications and SOAP/XML tooling. So it speaks XML, secures messages with XML Digital Signatures, and is delivered through the browser via HTTP redirects and form posts. It predates the mobile/API-first world that gave rise to OAuth and OpenID Connect — which is exactly why those newer protocols look so different (JSON, JWTs, bearer tokens).

Not obsolete

“Old” doesn’t mean “dead.” SAML remains the backbone of enterprise SSO and is deeply embedded in thousands of business applications. You’ll meet it for years to come — often alongside OAuth/OIDC.

Authentication, authorization & SSO

SAML primarily does authentication (“prove who you are”) and carries attributes about you (department, role, email). Apps then use those attributes to make their own authorization decisions. And by reusing one authentication across many apps, it delivers single sign-on.

TermQuestionSAML’s part
Authentication“Who are you?”✅ The IdP verifies you and asserts it
Attributes“What do we know about you?”✅ Carried in the assertion (e.g. role, email)
Authorization“What may you do?”The app decides, using those attributes
Single Sign-On“Do I log in again?”✅ No — one IdP session covers many apps

Key takeaways

  • SAML solves password & account sprawl across many applications.
  • It uses federation: identity lives in one trusted provider that vouches for you to apps.
  • Apps trust signed XML assertions instead of handling your password.
  • Result: single sign-on, central control, and consistent security.
